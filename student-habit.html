Overview

Project Background

This project investigates the relationship between student lifestyle habits and academic performance using survey data from 958 students spanning multiple education levels — High School, Bachelor, and Master. The analysis covers six behavioral dimensions: study hours, sleep duration, social media usage, dietary habits, physical exercise, and internet access state. The findings are presented through a single-page interactive Power BI dashboard with gender and age filters, enabling educators and researchers to explore how each habit cluster correlates with scores, attendance, and mental health.

Key Findings

What the Data Revealed

The dashboard surfaces six compelling patterns that connect daily student behavior to measurable academic and wellness outcomes.

📚
Diet Shapes Study Hours

Students with a Fair diet average 3.6 study hours per day — slightly higher than those with a Good (3.5h) or Poor (3.3h) diet. This counterintuitive finding suggests discipline in one area may reflect broader routine consistency.

😴
Sleep Is Nearly Equal by Job Status

Students with no part-time job sleep 6.46 hours on average, while working students sleep 6.45h — a negligible difference, suggesting sleep duration isn't significantly compromised by employment status.

🏋️
Exercise Declines with Education Level

High School students average the most exercise at 3.13 hours/week, with a steady decline through Bachelor (3.05h), Undeclared (2.99h), and Master students (2.88h) — reflecting rising academic pressure.

📱
Social Media Usage Is Consistent

Average social media hours are nearly identical across internet states — Good (2.6h), Poor (2.5h), and Average (2.4h) — indicating that internet quality has minimal effect on social media consumption habits.

⚧️
Gender Gap in Sleep vs. Study

Female students average 6.47 sleep hours and 3.58 study hours, while male students average 6.44 sleep and 3.51 study hours — a small but consistent pattern suggesting females invest slightly more in academic preparation.

🎯
Age 20 Has Peak Extracurricular Activity

Students aged 20 show the highest extracurricular participation at 139, followed by age 18 (127) and age 24 (127). The mid-undergraduate years appear to be the most socially and academically engaged period.

Methodology

How It Was Built

A clean, reproducible pipeline from raw survey data to a polished, filter-ready Power BI report.

01
Data Acquisition & Initial Assessment

Collected a structured student survey dataset covering 958 respondents across multiple education levels and demographics. Initial assessment identified key variables: age, gender, education level, diet quality, job status, internet state, study hours, sleep hours, social media usage, exercise frequency, attendance, mental health score, and academic score.

02
Data Cleaning with Power Query

Loaded the dataset into Power Query to handle missing values, standardize categorical labels (e.g., diet quality tiers, education levels), correct data types, and verify numeric ranges for hours-based columns. A clean, validated table was produced as the single source of truth for all visuals.

03
DAX Measures & Calculated Columns

Built DAX measures to calculate averages for mental health, attendance, academic score, study hours, sleep hours, and exercise — each segmented by gender, age, education level, diet, and job status. Calculated columns were added to enable behavioral groupings and cross-filter performance.

04
Dashboard Layout & Visual Design

Designed a single-page dashboard structured around six analytical panels: social media by internet state, study hours by diet, sleep by job status, exercise by education level, hours by gender, and extracurricular activity by age. KPI cards at the top surface headline numbers at a glance. Gender and age slicers enable real-time filtering across all visuals.

05
Insight Extraction & Storytelling

Reviewed all chart interactions to identify non-obvious behavioral patterns — particularly the diet-study relationship, the negligible sleep gap between working and non-working students, and the age-20 extracurricular peak. These findings were documented as actionable insights for educational stakeholders and researchers.
